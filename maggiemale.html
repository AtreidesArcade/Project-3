Male Analysis

In terms of male athletes, the number of male gold medalists have increased almost two-fold from 178 in 1960 to 340 in 2016. However, after the year 2000, there has been a general decrease in the number of male gold medalists. In 2012 for example, the number of gold medals dropped to 331, the lowest it has been in over 20 years. Perhaps, we suggest, GDP may not have as great of an effect on male athletes. From the scatter plot, we calculate a line of best fit of y = 58,888,617,568.98x + 394,568,915,890.89 and derive an R-value of 0.36. This suggests that there may not be as strong a correlation between GDP and number of gold medals won by male athletes. Indeed, after performing a T-Test, we were able to find that based on a p-value of 0.999 there was not a statistically significant difference in the percentage of gold medals won by male athletes compared to the total. We thus conclude that GDP does not play as big of a role on the performance of Olympic male athletes.

Female vs Male Analysis

Overall, the number of gold medalists have increased almost three-fold over the last 60 years from 211 in 1960 to 657 in 2016. Yet while the Olympics seemed to have gained popularity between 1960 and 1996, the total number of gold medalists have tapered off after the year 2000 with the number of gold medals won hovering around 660. More surprisingly, while the number of male gold medalists has increased just slightly over the last 60 years, the number of female gold medalists has exponentially sky rocketed. Perhaps one explanation for the sudden rise in female gold medalists is GDP. For our analysis, we created a scatter plot of number of gold medals won and GDP. From the scatter plot, we calculated a line of best fit of y = 45,589,473,771.98x + 284,954,880,795.76 and derived an R-value of 0.48. Based on this, we can see that there is a moderate correlation between the number of gold medals won and GDP. Thus, we conclude that GDP has a moderate effect on the number of gold medals a country receives.
